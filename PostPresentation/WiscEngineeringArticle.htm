Engineering Expo inspires K-12 students to explore engineering

Early Thursday afternoon at Engineering Expo, elementary and middle school students gathered around a glass display, behind which robotic hands moved unnaturally fast along a makeshift assembly line. Children crowded the area around the Engineering Centers Building entrance, excitedly asking questions about the robot and its functions.

Jane Feller, a representative for the Madison automation-assembly company Isthmus: Engineering and Manufacturing, explained that the robots are made to pick up objects along an assembly line with magnetic arms.

“Kids love the robots. They love seeing moving parts,” Feller said. “The big questions are what these robots are really for, and what’s picking the objects up.”

With a varied mixture of industry and student displays, the 2015 Engineering Expo, a biennial student-organized "open house" held April 14-17 on the UW-Madison engineering campus, offered everything from lessons on hydrophobic molecules to scientific explanations of toilet flushing. K-12 students wandered Engineering Mall on Thursday and Friday in a seemingly limitless search for their next learning adventure.

In Engineering Hall, students Alex Buehler, Jimmy Hermann and Pravin Yadab explained the benefits of a 3-D printer to passing children, who were fascinated by the technology.

“The kids from elementary school and middle school were just like, ‘This is amazing,’ Yadab said. “There were no stupid questions.”

Children were encouraged to take a short quiz on their knowledge, and if they scored well, they could receive a small souvenir, a tiny cube produced by the printer.

“Kids like it. It’s instant gratification,” Yadab said. “They have good questions, like whether you can print pizza with it.”

Many children were curious to learn how these types of technology function, as well as their cost. Such questions were ubiquitous at the event, as young students displayed an infinite curiosity and desire to learn.

Other displays were more industry-oriented, teaching kids the applications of science in everyday life. For instance, Kohler Engines representatives explained the mechanics of a touchless toilet flush, and acted as DJs for kids who wanted to try out the music-playing bathtub. A mechanical engineering student who co-ops with Kohler, Jessica Grev, enjoyed working with the kids, who showed a strong interest in and excitement for their surroundings.

“My favorite part is the kids’ reactions to some of the stuff,” she said. “They’re pretty fun. Three to four kids would get into the tub and request songs; I played ‘Fireworks’ by Katy Perry yesterday. They’re pretty interested.”

Expo also gives engineering students a chance to showcase their innovative ideas and engineering creations to a larger audience. Electrical engineering undergrad James Choncholas came up with a Pandora-like software called Miracle that uses the number-based qualities of song to classify and group music. As students watched the noise frequencies of “Uptown Funk”—an Expo favorite, according to Choncholas—flash across a computer screen, the computer was able to do mathematical operations to group this song with other, similar songs.

New ideas like Miracle are what give visitors a sense of what students are capable of through their university education, and how they use their own hobbies and interests to inform their learning.

Engineering students were able to use Expo as an opportunity to teach younger students what they’re doing at the university, creating a foundation for future interest in engineering fields. In the front lobby of the Mechanical Engineering Building, the Wisconsin Robotics Team allowed young kids to control a robot the team would have typically used for national competitions. While younger kids took advantage of this element, older students were more eager to learn the structural and technical components of the robots, according to student Stephen Eick.

“Some kids ask really good questions, like, ‘So this is a differential suspension you’re using here, right?’ I didn’t know that when I was 12,” Eick said.

As children wandered the halls of engineering on Friday afternoon, UW-Madison students reflected on the experience and the opportunity to impact young minds.

“It’s great to be able to help inspire them,” Eick said.

Lexy Brodt
